## The drop-off problem

Many technical posts are accurate but hard to finish. They bury the core argument under setup, context, and tangents.

I started tracking where readers stopped. The pattern was obvious: long openings and unclear section intent caused early drop-off.

## The structure I now use

Each post follows a predictable sequence:

1. State the problem in one short section
2. Explain constraints
3. Present the approach
4. Show tradeoffs
5. End with one practical takeaway

Readers can scan quickly and still understand the full argument.

## Clarity patterns that improved completion

- Short paragraphs with one idea each
- Section headings that communicate outcome, not topic
- Bullet lists for decision criteria
- Minimal jargon unless it unlocks precision

This improved readability without reducing technical depth.

## The editorial test

Before publishing, I ask:

- Can a reader understand the key point after the first two sections?
- Is there at least one reusable mental model?
- Did I include real tradeoffs, not generic advice?

If the answer is no, the draft is not ready.

## Closing thought

Good technical writing is product design. It reduces cognitive load, respects reader time, and helps people make better decisions.

If a post cannot guide action, it is documentation noise.
