## The issue with most side projects

Many projects show effort but not engineering maturity. They often demonstrate implementation, yet skip tradeoffs, constraints, and long-term thinking.

Hiring teams look for signal, not just screenshots.

## Three things that improve signal quality

### 1. Show constraints explicitly

A project becomes credible when you describe real constraints:

- budget limits
- performance targets
- device compatibility
- maintenance costs

This shows that decisions were intentional, not accidental.

### 2. Explain architecture at the right level

Avoid overloading readers with diagrams that explain everything. Focus on the few decisions that drove outcomes.

For example:

- Why server rendering instead of full client rendering
- Why static generation for content routes
- Why a strict metadata model for blog entries

Clear architecture narratives make interviews easier.

### 3. Document the tradeoffs you accepted

Every meaningful system has compromises. If you can explain what you chose not to build and why, your project reads like senior-level work.

## A useful project story template

I structure project writeups in this order:

1. Problem and context
2. Constraints
3. Chosen approach
4. Tradeoffs
5. Results and next iteration

This format is simple, but it translates well in portfolio pages and technical interviews.

## Final note

A side project is not judged by size. It is judged by clarity of thought and quality of decisions under constraints.

That is the signal worth optimizing for.
